What Is Energy, Really?

Energy is the capacity to do work — to power factories, heat homes, move vehicles, and run data centers. The global energy system is the largest industrial enterprise in human history. As of 2024, humanity consumed about 650 exajoules (EJ) of energy per year — roughly equivalent to burning 155 billion barrels of oil.

Energy comes in two fundamental roles: primary energy (what we extract from nature — coal, oil, uranium, sunlight) and final energy (what reaches the consumer after conversion and transmission losses). About 30–40% of primary energy is lost in conversion.

Why This Matters for You

The price of energy underpins everything: food prices (fertilisers need gas), transport costs, manufacturing, heating. When energy prices spike — as they did after the Ukraine war in 2022 — inflation affects every household on earth. When they fall, economies breathe. Understanding energy is understanding the modern world.

⚡ All Energy Types Explained

From ancient coal to cutting-edge fusion — how each type works, where it comes from, and its place in the global mix.

🪨 Coal

Coal is fossilised plant matter compressed over millions of years. It's burned in power stations to heat water, create steam, and spin turbines. There are four grades: lignite (brown coal, lowest energy), sub-bituminous, bituminous, and anthracite (highest energy, cleanest burn).

Where: China burns 67% of the world's coal. India is second. Poland and Germany are the EU's last major coal users. Australia, Indonesia, and Russia are the biggest exporters.

Climate impact: Coal releases roughly 820–1,000g CO₂ per kWh — twice that of natural gas. It's the single largest contributor to climate change.

⚠️ Coal in 2024
Despite all transition pledges, coal hit a record global demand of 169 QBtu in 2024, up 3.2% per year since 2020. The reason: developing Asia, especially India and Southeast Asia, still builds coal plants for cheap electricity to industrialise.

🛢️ Oil / Petroleum

Oil is crude petroleum — a mixture of hydrocarbons extracted from underground reservoirs. It's refined into gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, heating oil, and petrochemicals (plastics, fertilisers). Unlike coal and gas (mostly used for electricity), oil dominates transport — powering 90% of all vehicles globally.

Extraction methods: conventional drilling, deep-sea drilling (Gulf of Mexico, North Sea), horizontal hydraulic fracturing ("fracking" — responsible for the US shale revolution), oil sands (Canada's Alberta).

Key producers: USA (13.6 Mb/day — #1 in 2025), Russia (9.9), Saudi Arabia (9.5), Canada (4.9), Iraq (4.4), China (4.3).

Price drivers: OPEC+ production decisions, geopolitical crises (wars, sanctions), dollar strength, US shale output, and global economic growth.

🔥 Natural Gas

Natural gas is primarily methane (CH₄), found in underground deposits often alongside oil. It's used for electricity generation, industrial heating, cooking, and as a chemical feedstock. It's the "cleanest" fossil fuel — emitting roughly 450g CO₂/kWh, about half of coal.

LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas): Gas is cooled to −162°C to become liquid, shrinking to 1/600th its volume for shipping. LNG tankers enabled a global gas market where previously gas was landlocked by pipelines. The US became the world's largest LNG exporter by 2023.

The price disconnect: US gas (Henry Hub) trades at ~$3–4/MMBtu while European gas (TTF) trades at €30–50/MWh because Europe lost Russian pipeline gas and must compete for LNG cargoes globally.

☢️ Nuclear Energy

Nuclear power splits heavy atoms (usually uranium-235 or plutonium-239) in a process called fission. This releases enormous heat, which boils water to drive turbines. Nuclear produces zero CO₂ emissions during operation, runs 24/7 at ~90%+ capacity factor, and generates enormous energy density — one kilogram of uranium fuel contains as much energy as ~3,000 tonnes of coal.

Types of reactors: Pressurised Water Reactors (PWRs, most common), Boiling Water Reactors (BWRs), CANDU (heavy water), Fast Breeder Reactors, and coming: Small Modular Reactors (SMRs).

Countries leading: France (70% of electricity from nuclear), USA (largest fleet by output), China (aggressive expansion — world leader in new builds), South Korea, Canada.

☢️ The Nuclear Renaissance
After Fukushima (2011) caused a decade-long retreat, nuclear is back. The EU re-classified it as "green." Microsoft signed a deal to restart Three Mile Island. France is building new reactors. China is building faster than the rest of the world combined. SMRs promise lower costs.

☀️ Solar PV (Photovoltaic)

Solar panels use the photovoltaic effect — photons of light knock electrons loose in silicon semiconductors, creating direct current (DC) electricity. An inverter converts this to AC for the grid. Solar panels have no moving parts, no fuel, near-zero operating cost, and a 25–30 year lifespan.

Types: Monocrystalline silicon (most efficient, ~22%), Polycrystalline (~18%), Thin-film (flexible, cheaper), Bifacial (captures light on both sides), Perovskite (next-gen, under development).

The cost revolution: Solar PV costs have fallen over 90% in the past 15 years. In 2010, solar cost ~$400/MWh. By 2025, utility-scale solar in sunny regions costs $37–55/MWh — cheaper than any fossil fuel plant ever built.

Limitation: It only generates when the sun shines. This intermittency requires backup (batteries, gas, pumped hydro) and makes the grid more complex to manage. Negative electricity prices (you pay the grid to take electricity) now occur frequently in very sunny or windy periods.

💨 Wind Energy

Wind turbines convert kinetic energy of moving air into electricity. Blades turn a rotor → gearbox → generator. Modern turbines stand 100–200m tall with blade spans up to 270m (offshore). Wind is the single largest source of renewable electricity in the EU and in many US states.

Onshore vs Offshore: Onshore is cheaper (~$37–86/MWh). Offshore costs more ($72–140/MWh) but winds are stronger and more consistent, and turbines can be much larger. UK, Germany, Denmark, and China lead in offshore wind.

💧 Hydropower

Hydropower harnesses the potential energy of water falling from height. Dams store water; it's released through turbines. It provides reliable, dispatchable, low-cost electricity and can act as a giant "battery" (pumped storage hydro) by pumping water uphill when electricity is cheap and releasing it when expensive.

Largest plants: Three Gorges Dam (China, 22.5 GW), Itaipu (Brazil/Paraguay, 14 GW), Belo Monte (Brazil). Brazil generates ~65% of electricity from hydro. Norway and Iceland nearly 100%.

🌿 Bioenergy

Burning biological material (wood, agricultural waste, purpose-grown crops, landfill gas, biogas from organic waste). It's the largest renewable energy source in the EU by volume — mostly wood for heating. Controversial: large-scale biomass burning (like UK's Drax power station, which burns wood pellets shipped from North America) is disputed as genuinely "carbon neutral."

🌋 Geothermal & Others

Geothermal taps heat from the earth's crust. Iceland gets ~66% of its primary energy from geothermal and hydro combined. Kenya, the Philippines, and parts of the US (California, Nevada) use it. Tidal and wave power remain small-scale experiments. Hydrogen is growing as an industrial feedstock and potential fuel but is an energy carrier, not a source.

💰 Energy Prices & the Cost of Electricity

Understanding LCOE — the true cost of electricity from any source — and how it compares globally.

What Is LCOE?

Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE) is the average cost per unit of electricity over a plant's lifetime, accounting for construction costs, financing, fuel, operations, and decommissioning. It's the most common way to compare energy sources fairly — even if they have wildly different cost structures (all upfront vs. all ongoing).

🏭 How Energy Is Produced

From mine to megawatt — the full chain of energy production.

The Universal Principle: Heat → Steam → Electricity

About 80% of global electricity is generated through the same basic principle: burn/react something to make heat → heat water to steam → steam spins a turbine → turbine spins a generator. This applies to coal, gas, nuclear, and even concentrated solar (CSP). The fuel source differs; the machinery is essentially the same.

⚙️ The Three Exceptions
Solar PV, wind, and hydropower do NOT use heat. Solar PV uses the photoelectric effect directly. Wind uses mechanical rotation. Hydro uses gravity and water movement. These are fundamentally different and have much lower conversion losses.

Coal-Fired Power Plants

Coal is crushed into powder, blown into a boiler, and burned at 1,300°C+. This heats water in tubes surrounding the boiler into high-pressure steam. The steam spins a turbine connected to a generator. Efficiency of modern supercritical coal plants: ~42–46%. Older plants: 30–35%. The remainder is lost as waste heat.

Gas-Fired Plants (CCGT)

Combined Cycle Gas Turbines (CCGT) are the most efficient thermal plants. Natural gas is burned in a jet-engine-like turbine (first cycle). The hot exhaust gas then heats a steam boiler for a second steam cycle. Total efficiency: up to 60–62%. Gas plants are also fast-starting — key for backing up intermittent renewables.

Nuclear Reactors

Uranium fuel pellets (ceramic, fingernail-sized) are stacked in fuel rods. Control rods (boron or hafnium) absorb neutrons to control the rate of fission. In a Pressurised Water Reactor (PWR): water under high pressure absorbs heat from the reactor core (primary circuit) → transfers heat through a heat exchanger to a secondary circuit → that water becomes steam → drives turbines. The radioactive primary water never touches the turbine.

One kg of uranium fuel = 45,000 kWh of electricity. One kg of coal = ~3 kWh. This 15,000x difference in energy density is why nuclear power produces so little waste by volume.

Solar PV Panels — How They're Made & How They Work

Silicon is purified to 99.9999% purity (from ordinary sand/quartz). It's grown into large crystals, sliced into wafers ~180 micrometers thin, treated with phosphorus (n-type layer) and boron (p-type layer) to create a p-n junction. When photons hit this junction, they free electrons, creating current. Multiple cells → panel → array → inverter → grid.

Manufacturing is dominated by China, which controls ~80-90% of global solar panel production. This creates supply chain dependency that is increasingly a geopolitical concern for the EU and US.

Wind Turbines

Blades (fibreglass/carbon composite, up to 107m long) rotate at 5–15 RPM. A gearbox multiplies this to 1,500 RPM for the generator. Modern turbines have variable-pitch blades that turn to face the wind (yaw system) and feather into the wind when it's too strong. Power output scales with the cube of wind speed — doubling wind speed gives 8x the power. The largest offshore turbines today (like Siemens Gamesa's 14MW or GE Haliade-X 15MW) can power ~14,000 homes each.

The Grid: Keeping the Lights On

The electricity grid is arguably the most complex machine ever built. It must balance supply and demand in real time — frequency deviation by more than 1% can damage equipment or cause blackouts. Grid operators use a "merit order" — cheapest generation runs first (renewables), then gas, then peaking plants — to minimise cost. With more intermittent renewables, grids need more flexibility: battery storage, pumped hydro, demand response, and interconnectors between regions.

The 2024 EU Electricity Milestone

In 2024, the EU achieved its cleanest electricity mix in decades. Clean sources (renewables + nuclear) reached 71% of all electricity. Solar surpassed coal for the first time ever — providing 11% (304 TWh) vs coal's 9.8% (269 TWh). Wind + solar together hit a record 29%. Fossil fuels fell to their lowest level in over 40 years.

The Russia-Ukraine Energy Shock in Europe

Before February 2022, Russia supplied ~40% of Europe's gas and ~25% of its oil. The invasion transformed European energy policy overnight. Gas prices surged from ~€20/MWh to €350/MWh (August 2022) — a 17x spike. European governments spent an estimated €800 billion+ on energy support measures to shield households and businesses.

The EU's response — REPowerEU — accelerated renewable deployment, diversified LNG imports (from US, Qatar, Norway), built regasification terminals in record time, and cut gas demand by ~15%. By 2024, the EU reduced Russian gas from 40% to under 8% of supply. The crisis effectively turbocharged the green transition by making energy independence a security priority, not just a climate one.

EU Electricity Market Design

The EU uses a "marginal pricing" system: electricity is priced at the cost of the last (most expensive) plant needed to meet demand. When renewables flood the market, prices crash (or go negative). When gas turbines are needed, all producers — including cheap renewables — earn the high gas price. This created massive windfall profits for renewable operators and nuclear plants during the 2022 gas crisis, while consumers suffered. The EU reformed this system in 2024 with new contracts-for-difference mechanisms to decouple renewable prices from gas.

Why energy prices are really a story of wars, politics, pandemics, and power.

US shale oil/gas production explodes thanks to horizontal drilling + hydraulic fracturing. By 2014, US oil output exceeds Saudi Arabia's. Many shale companies go bankrupt — but the industry survives, restructured. The US becomes the world's largest oil producer.

COVID-19 Pandemic — The Unprecedented Demand Collapse
Lockdowns worldwide destroy demand for oil virtually overnight. In April 2020, US oil futures briefly went negative (−$37/barrel) — meaning producers paid people to take oil because storage was completely full. Global energy demand fell by 4% — the largest single-year drop ever. Oil price war between Saudi Arabia and Russia worsened the glut. Coal, gas, and electricity prices all cratered. Renewables were the only sector that continued growing. The pandemic also revealed the fragility of global supply chains, laying groundwork for post-pandemic inflation.

Economies reopen faster than energy supply can respond. Underinvestment in fossil fuels during COVID, combined with a cold winter and low wind/hydro output, causes an energy crunch. Gas prices in Europe begin rising sharply. Electricity prices follow. Coal demand surges as gas becomes expensive. First warning signs of Europe's vulnerability to Russian gas.

Russia Invades Ukraine — The Most Severe Energy Shock Since 1973
Russia cuts gas supplies to Europe (first partially, then massively through deliberate sabotage arguments). European TTF gas hits €350/MWh — a 17x increase from 2021 norms. Oil briefly tops $130/barrel. European electricity prices become the most expensive in the world. Inflation surges to 8–11% across Europe. EU governments spend ~€800 billion in energy support measures. Germany reconsidered closing its last nuclear plants (briefly extended). The crisis permanently reshaped the geopolitics of energy: Europe's energy dependence on Russia became its greatest strategic vulnerability. The energy system's exposure to geopolitical risk became undeniable.

Price Normalisation, but With New Vulnerabilities
Energy prices moderate. EU has diversified gas supply, built LNG terminals, and filled storage. But European gas prices remain 2–3x US prices — a permanent competitiveness disadvantage. Middle East conflict (Israel-Gaza, Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping) disrupts trade routes. EU industries increasingly relocate to the US (cheap energy + IRA subsidies) in a phenomenon called "deindustrialisation." The global energy commodity price index fell from its 2022 peak but remains above pre-COVID levels.

OPEC+ and the Global Oil Cartel

OPEC (Organisation of Petroleum Exporting Countries) was founded in 1960 by Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Venezuela. Today OPEC+ includes Russia, Kazakhstan, and others — controlling about 40% of global oil output and 80% of proven reserves. When OPEC+ cuts production, prices rise. When they flood the market, prices fall. Their decisions are explicitly geopolitical — rewarding allies, punishing adversaries, funding state budgets.

Saudi Arabia needs oil at ~$70–80/barrel to balance its state budget. Russia needs it at ~$60–70. The US shale industry breaks even at ~$40–50. These "fiscal breakevens" create the real floor for oil prices.

The Petrodollar System

Since 1973, oil has been traded globally in US dollars. This means every country needs dollars to buy oil — creating permanent global demand for the dollar, underpinning US financial hegemony. Any attempt to price oil in other currencies (yuan, euro) is a direct challenge to US power. Saudi Arabia has recently begun exploring yuan-denominated oil trades with China — watched closely by geopolitical analysts.

Energy Sanctions as a Geopolitical Weapon

Western sanctions on Russia cut off its access to oil service technology, limiting production growth. Russian oil is now sold at a discount (the "Urals discount") to India, China, and Turkey. Iran has been under oil sanctions for decades — yet still exports oil covertly. Venezuela's production collapsed partly due to sanctions and partly due to regime mismanagement. Energy sanctions are powerful but blunt instruments with widespread civilian impacts.

The AI & Data Center Energy Boom (2024–)

🤖 A New Energy Shock: AI
Data center electricity demand is expected to double between 2024 and 2030, driven primarily by AI. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta are signing nuclear power deals at scale (including restarting Three Mile Island). The US PJM grid region (serving 65 million people) saw capacity auction prices spike 800% in 2024 due to data center demand. The AI revolution is reversing decades of efficiency improvements in energy intensity.

🔮 The Future of Energy

Where we are, where we're going, and what it means.

The Energy Trilemma

Every country must balance three competing energy goals simultaneously — the energy trilemma:

🌿
Sustainability
Decarbonising to limit climate change. Net zero by 2050 requires unprecedented speed.
🔒
Security
Ensuring reliable supply, reducing import dependence (as Europe learned painfully in 2022).
💶
Affordability
Keeping energy affordable for households and businesses — especially in developing nations.

Where Is the World Going?

The evidence is clear: the energy transition is real, rapid, and irreversible. In 2024, renewables accounted for 38% of global energy supply growth, ahead of any fossil fuel. Solar and wind grew 16%. China alone added twice the renewable capacity of the US + EU + India combined.

However, total fossil fuel demand is still rising in absolute terms, because global energy demand is growing faster than renewables can displace existing use. The 1.5°C climate goal is now considered by most analysts to be out of reach. The realistic target is staying below 2°C, requiring massive acceleration of the transition.

Grid storage ~1,500 GWh globally 50x increase by 2040 to handle solar/wind intermittency

The New Geopolitical Contest

The 20th century's energy geopolitics was about controlling oil fields. The 21st century's will be about controlling the clean energy supply chain: solar panels (China ~85%), batteries (China ~75%), wind turbines, and critical minerals (lithium from Chile/Australia/Argentina, cobalt from Congo, rare earths from China). The US, EU, and others are trying to de-risk these supply chains through the IRA, European Green Deal, and trade policies — with limited success so far.

🌟 The Optimistic View
Solar costs fell 90% in 15 years. Battery costs fell 97% since 1991. Wind costs halved. The cost curves are still declining. Renewables are now cheaper than any fossil fuel in most of the world. The energy transition, for the first time, is not just an environmental imperative — it's economically rational. Countries that lead it will have cheaper energy, more energy independence, and new industries.
⚠️ The Sobering Reality
Fossil fuels still provide 86% of global energy. Coal hit record demand in 2024. Global emissions exceeded 40 GtCO₂e. The energy system is immense and changes slowly. Even in the most optimistic scenarios, oil and gas will still be significant in 2050. The transition requires not just technology but political will, financing, and international cooperation — all under severe strain.

Technologies to Watch

⚛️
Small Modular Reactors (SMRs)
Factory-built nuclear units of 50–300 MW. Could decarbonise industrial heat and remote grids. First commercial SMRs expected 2028–2032. Still more expensive than solar/wind but offer 24/7 clean power.
In development
🔋
Grid-Scale Batteries
4-hour, 8-hour, and soon 24-hour batteries solve solar/wind intermittency. Costs fell 40%+ in 2024 alone. Australia leading real-world deployment.
Rapidly scaling
🌊
Green Hydrogen
Electrolysis splits water into hydrogen using renewable electricity. Can decarbonise shipping, steel, chemicals. Still expensive. Projects emerging in Australia, Chile, Namibia, Saudi Arabia.
Expensive but promising
☀️
Perovskite Solar
Next-generation solar cells, potentially 30%+ efficiency at very low cost. Could make solar even cheaper. Commercialisation expected in the late 2020s.
Near-term breakthrough
🌋
Enhanced Geothermal
Drilling deep enough anywhere to access geothermal heat — not just volcanic regions. Quaise Energy and others pursuing this. If successful, near-unlimited 24/7 clean power anywhere on earth.
Long-term potential
🌟
Nuclear Fusion
Joins atoms instead of splitting them. Zero waste, effectively unlimited fuel (hydrogen). NIF achieved ignition in 2022 (energy out > energy in). Commercial fusion: 2040s at earliest, if ever. The "always 20 years away" joke may finally be becoming wrong.
Long-term wildcard
