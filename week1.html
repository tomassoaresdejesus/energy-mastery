Topic 01

PRIMARY VS
FINAL ENERGY

The distinction most journalists and politicians get wrong — and why it completely changes how you read energy statistics.

What Is Primary Energy?

Primary energy is energy as it exists in nature, before any human transformation. It's the raw input: coal sitting in a mine, crude oil underground, uranium in a fuel rod, wind blowing across a field, sunlight hitting a panel. When you read "the world consumed 650 EJ of energy in 2024" — that's primary energy. It's the total of everything we extracted or captured from nature.

What Is Final Energy?

Final energy is what actually reaches the end user — the energy that does useful work for people and industry: the electricity that powers your laptop, the diesel that moves a truck, the gas that heats your home, the petrol that propels your car.

The Gap — And Why It's Huge

A massive amount of primary energy is lost before it becomes final energy. This is called conversion loss or thermodynamic loss. The main culprit is heat engines. When you burn coal in a power plant to make electricity, you're boiling water with fire to spin a turbine — a process that is fundamentally inefficient. The best modern coal plants convert about 45% of the coal's energy into electricity. The other 55% escapes as waste heat.

Key Physics
This isn't an engineering flaw that hasn't been fixed yet. It's the Second Law of Thermodynamics — the Carnot efficiency ceiling. Any heat engine is fundamentally limited by the temperature difference between its hot and cold reservoirs. No amount of better engineering can beat it.

Why This Matters — The EV Revolution Explained

Electric vehicles are roughly 85–90% efficient (battery to wheels). Compare that to 20–25% for a petrol engine. Even if your electricity comes from a gas power plant (~55–60% efficient), the full chain is still roughly comparable to a petrol car. If your electricity comes from renewables — the comparison is no contest.

The Statistics Trap
When someone says "renewables are 14% of global energy," they mean 14% of primary energy. But solar and wind skip the thermal conversion stage — they convert energy directly. Their useful contribution is proportionally higher than the primary energy number suggests. This is why the IEA uses the "substitution method" in some of its reporting.
The Big Insight
A fully electrified economy running on renewables might need only 40–50% of today's primary energy to deliver the same standard of living. The energy transition isn't just swapping fuels — it's a massive efficiency upgrade for the whole economy.

Why Each Fuel Dominates Its Sector

Oil — owns transport
Oil is energy-dense, liquid at room temperature, stable, and surrounded by 150 years of infrastructure (pipelines, tankers, petrol stations). About 90% of all transport energy globally is oil. There are 1.4 billion internal combustion engine vehicles on the road right now. They won't be retired in a decade.
Coal — owns cheap electricity & steel
Coal is the most abundant fossil fuel on earth, found almost everywhere, cheap to mine and burn. For a developing country that needs to industrialise fast, coal is the fastest and cheapest path. China and India between them burn about 73% of the world's coal. Coal is also still essential for steelmaking via coking coal — a process with no fully commercial clean alternative yet.
Gas — the flexible backbone
Gas is cleaner than coal, ramps up and down faster (critical for grid balancing), and is the feedstock for fertilisers via the Haber-Bosch process. Without gas-derived fertilisers, the world could feed roughly 4 billion people — not 8 billion. Natural gas is literally keeping half of humanity alive.

Topic 03

NATURAL GAS
& LNG

The technology that broke the geopolitical stranglehold of pipelines — and created a new global market for the fuel that powers industrial civilisation.

The Three Jobs of Natural Gas

1 · Electricity Generation (~40% of gas use)
Gas-fired Combined Cycle Gas Turbines (CCGTs) are the most efficient thermal power plants ever built — up to 62% efficiency. They start and stop in minutes, making them perfect for balancing intermittent solar and wind. This is why gas is called the "transition fuel."
2 · Industrial Heat & Fertiliser (~35%)
The Haber-Bosch process uses gas-derived hydrogen to synthesise ammonia for fertiliser. Without this process, the world can feed ~4 billion people. With it, 8 billion. Natural gas is the invisible foundation of the global food supply.
3 · Heating Buildings (~25%)
Gas boilers heat homes across northern Europe, North America, and Russia — creating massive seasonal demand swings that drive the entire complexity of gas storage and supply security.

This price disconnect exists because gas is hard to move. Oil is a liquid — put it in a tanker and ship it anywhere. Gas is a gas at room temperature — you can't pour it into a ship. This is where LNG changes everything.

What Is LNG?

LNG = Liquefied Natural Gas. Same methane molecule, cooled to −162°C (−260°F), at which point it becomes liquid and shrinks to 1/600th of its original volume. One cubic metre of LNG contains 600 cubic metres of gas. Suddenly, transporting gas across oceans in ships becomes economically viable. LNG broke pipeline monopolies and created competition in gas markets for the first time.

Germany's Emergency Response — 18 Months
After Russia cut gas flows in 2022, Germany went from zero LNG terminals to five in under 18 months using Floating Storage and Regasification Units (FSRUs) — essentially LNG terminals built on ships, deployed in 9 months instead of 7 years. An extraordinary feat of industrial emergency response.
Spain — The Quiet Winner
Spain has 6 regasification terminals, built decades ago because it had no pipeline connection to Russia. This is why Spain sailed through the 2022 energy crisis with relatively lower prices — it could receive LNG from anywhere in the world.

The Critical Systems Insight
Every renewable except hydro and geothermal is intermittent. At 30–40% renewable share, system integration costs are manageable. At 80–100%, they become the central challenge of energy economics — requiring massive storage buildout, grid expansion, and interconnectors. This is why nuclear, hydro, and geothermal are strategically valuable beyond their raw output numbers: they provide clean power whenever you need it, regardless of weather.
Topic 05

WHY THE WORLD
STILL RUNS ON 86%
FOSSIL FUELS

Renewables are growing at 16% per year. Solar and wind are now the cheapest electricity ever built. So why hasn't the number moved? Here are the six reasons — updated to April 2026.

The Stubborn Number
The Energy Institute's 2025 Statistical Review confirmed fossil fuels still account for 86% of the global energy mix. Renewables added to the mix in 2025 — but they did not meaningfully replace what was already there. The headline figure has barely moved in a decade.
1

Infrastructure Lock-In

The global economy is physically built around fossil fuel equipment with decades of useful life remaining. 1.4 billion petrol cars. Millions of gas boilers. Thousands of coal power stations. China approved nearly 100 GW of new coal plants in 2024 alone — plants designed to operate for 30–40 years. These are not temporary measures. They are deliberate long-term commitments being made right now.

2

Energy Density & Reliability

Fossil fuels are energy-dense, storable for months at low cost, and available 24/7 regardless of weather. When wind speeds dropped across Europe in early 2025, gas generation rose 8% to compensate, pushing the EU's gas import bill up by €32 billion for the year. One bad weather season can undo months of renewable progress.

3

Cost Asymmetry Across Sectors

In electricity, the economic battle is over — renewables have won. But electricity is roughly one-sixth of all energy. Transport, industrial heat, and heating still run on fuels, not electrons. In each case, the transition requires replacing not just the fuel but the entire end-use technology — buy a new car, rebuild a factory, replace a gas boiler. That costs trillions and takes decades.

4

Political Economy & Vested Interests

The US is the clearest current example. Coal generation jumped 12–13% in 2025. Power sector emissions rose 4.4% — the second consecutive year of increases. The Trump administration propped up coal with federal emergency orders. The IEA revised US renewable forecasts down by nearly 50% for 2025–2030. A country that was leading the clean transition is now actively reversing it. The transition is not just a technology problem — it is a political one.

5

The Developing World's Legitimate Claim

Rich nations industrialised on cheap coal and oil over 150 years and became wealthy. Now they ask India, Africa, and Southeast Asia to develop using more expensive clean energy. Africa accounts for only 2% of global clean energy investment despite 20% of the world's population. Africa's total energy investment in 2025 is one-third lower than it was in 2015. When a Nigerian government chooses between reliable power now or a solar project it cannot finance, it chooses gas. That is not a failure of values — it is a rational response to an unequal global financial system.

6

The Speed Gap — And AI as a New Wildcard

Wind and solar expanded by 16% in 2024 — yet total fossil fuel use still grew. Renewables are winning the race for new capacity but haven't peaked total fossil demand. And now there is a new accelerant: data centres consumed ~415 TWh globally in 2024 (+12%/yr since 2017). The IEA projects data centre demand to more than double to 945 TWh by 2030 — roughly Japan's entire current electricity consumption. In the US, data centres will account for nearly half of all electricity demand growth through 2030. In the short term, natural gas is meeting most of this new demand. AI is making the transition harder, not easier.
